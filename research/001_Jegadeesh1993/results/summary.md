# Replication Summary — Jegadeesh & Titman (1993)

## Objective

This project replicates the main empirical findings of *Returns to Buying Winners and Selling Losers: Implications for Stock Market Efficiency* (Jegadeesh & Titman, 1993) using Yahoo Finance data on current S&P 500 constituents from January 2015 to December 2025.

Unlike the original paper, which uses CRSP data covering NYSE/AMEX stocks between 1965 and 1989, this replication focuses on a modern equity universe to examine whether the empirical patterns remain present in today's market.

---

# 1. Momentum Strategy Replication (Table I)

All combinations of formation periods (J = 3, 6, 9, 12 months) and holding periods (K = 3, 6, 9, 12 months) were tested using equal-weighted decile portfolios.

## Main findings

* Momentum remains profitable in the modern sample.
* The best-performing strategy is **6-month formation / 3-month holding (6/3)**.
* Momentum profitability gradually decreases as the holding period becomes longer.
* Formation periods longer than 9–12 months produce substantially weaker returns.

Compared with the original paper, where 12/3 performs best, the optimal formation window appears shorter in the modern market.

Possible explanations include:

* faster information diffusion,
* higher market efficiency,
* broader participation of quantitative investors.

This observation is treated as an extension rather than a conclusion of the original paper.

---

# 2. Beta Characteristics (Table II)

Post-ranking CAPM betas were estimated for each decile portfolio.

Main observations:

* Loser portfolios exhibit higher market beta than winner portfolios.
* Winner portfolios generate higher average returns despite having lower beta.

This result suggests that momentum profits are unlikely to be explained solely by exposure to systematic market risk.

---

# 3. Beta-Subsample Test (Table III)

Stocks were first divided into Low-, Medium-, and High-beta groups using rolling beta estimates.

Momentum strategies were then constructed independently within each beta group.

Results:

| Beta Group  | Monthly Long-Short Return |
| ----------- | ------------------------: |
| Low Beta    |                    +0.44% |
| Medium Beta |                       ~0% |
| High Beta   |                    +0.77% |

The momentum effect does not disappear after controlling for beta exposure.

Therefore, cross-sectional differences in expected returns cannot fully explain the observed momentum profits.

---

# 4. Common-Factor Tests (Section III)

## Market Serial Dependence

Monthly market returns exhibit mild mean reversion.

However, six-month cumulative market returns display positive serial dependence in the modern sample, differing from the original CRSP evidence.

## Lead-Lag Regression

Momentum profits were regressed on lagged squared market returns.

Estimated coefficient:

* Negative
* Statistically insignificant

Therefore, the data do not support the hypothesis that momentum profits are primarily generated by delayed reactions to common market shocks.

---

# 5. Event-Time Analysis (Section VI)

Each momentum portfolio was tracked for 36 months after formation.

Results show three distinct stages.

### Stage 1 (Months 1–14)

Momentum profits continue to accumulate after portfolio formation.

Peak cumulative long-short return occurs around Month 14.

### Stage 2 (Months 14–24)

Momentum gradually weakens.

Portfolio returns begin to converge.

### Stage 3 (Months 24–36)

The winner-minus-loser portfolio turns negative.

A long-term reversal pattern emerges.

Although the exact timing differs from the original paper, the overall shape is remarkably similar:

* short-term continuation,
* followed by long-term reversal.

This remains one of the strongest pieces of evidence supporting the delayed-information interpretation of momentum.

---

# Overall Conclusion

Using modern S&P 500 data, the main qualitative conclusions of Jegadeesh & Titman (1993) continue to hold.

Specifically:

* Momentum remains economically profitable.
* Systematic risk alone cannot fully explain momentum profits.
* Controlling for beta does not eliminate the momentum effect.
* Common-factor dynamics provide limited explanatory power.
* Event-time performance exhibits short-term continuation followed by long-term reversal.

The primary difference from the original study lies in the optimal strategy parameters.

While Jegadeesh & Titman (1993) report strongest performance for a 12/3 strategy, the modern sample favors a shorter 6/3 specification, suggesting that information may be incorporated into prices more rapidly in contemporary equity markets.

---

# Limitations

* Current S&P 500 constituents introduce survivorship bias.
* Yahoo Finance is used instead of CRSP.
* Equal-weighted market returns are used as a proxy for the market factor.
* Historical market capitalization data are unavailable, preventing a full replication of the size-based subsample tests.
